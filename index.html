Reading the map

A continent of empty interior — and one persistent outlier.

Year after year, Northern Territory and Western Australia dominate the detection count. The NT’s tropical savanna burns annually by design: Indigenous land managers have practised seasonal burning for millennia, and contemporary programs integrate that Traditional Ecological Knowledge with satellite monitoring.

Since 2013, NT Indigenous-led prescribed burns have reduced late dry-season wildfires by an area equivalent to 115 000 km². The NT burns often — but without that management, it would burn worse.

Source · NASA FIRMS · MODIS + VIIRS · 2014–2024

The monsoon rhythm

Wet suppresses. Dry ignites.

The NT sits in a tropical monsoon belt. Nearly 90% of annual rainfall falls between November and April. When the wet season ends, grasses cure rapidly into dense fuel loads, and fire counts climb steeply from May onward.

In 2023 — the decade’s record year with 136,961 NT hotspot detections — September recorded just 0.4 mm of rain (seventh driest on record) while temperatures averaged 2.08 °C above the 1961–1990 mean. The season was extreme in degree, not in shape.

Source · NASA FIRMS · NT Monthly Hotspot Dataset · 2014–2024

Seasonal pattern

The dry season is the fire season.

Across all eleven years, roughly 75–80% of NT hotspot detections fall between June and November. August through October are the dominant months in every row — a structural signal that does not change with the calendar.

What does change is scale. The 2023 row is exceptionally bright: nearly double any prior year. An unprecedented September heat event (0.4 mm rain; +2.1 °C anomaly) compressed an already intense dry-season into a record-breaking single month. 2023 did not invent the pattern — it amplified it to a new extreme.

Geography

Location is constant. Intensity is the year-to-year story.

Fires consistently concentrate across the Top End, Arnhem Land, and the Barkly Tablelands — tropical and semi-arid savanna country where seasonal burning is a structural feature of land management, not an accident.

Early dry-season burns (April–July), practised by Indigenous rangers under traditional ecological knowledge, create firebreaks that limit the scale of late-season fires. The dot colours tell the consequence: years with coordinated early burning show fewer extreme-FRP detections than years where late dry-season conditions escape control.

Intensity split

High-intensity fires are rare — but carry outsized impact.

In most years, detections above 50 MW account for fewer than 20% of the total — yet each high-FRP event releases roughly ten times the radiative energy per area of a low-intensity fire, with disproportionate consequences for smoke emissions and ecosystem recovery time.

The 2023 bar stands apart on both sides: record low- and high-intensity counts. The spike was not simply more fires; it was more fires of every kind, driven by an exceptional late dry-season heat event across the Barkly Tablelands and Tanami Desert.

When do fires burn?

Time of day · rendering
Daily fire window

Afternoon heat drives the detection peak — every year.

The 12:00–15:59 UTC window (approximately 21:30–01:29 ACST) accounts for the largest share of daily hotspot detections in almost every year. Peak solar heating lowers fuel moisture and accelerates fire spread, making this the highest-risk window of the day.

Late night and early morning (00:00–07:59 UTC) record the fewest detections. Overnight cooling and rising humidity suppress active burning, even where fire fronts remain smouldering beneath the surface. The pattern holds consistently across the decade — years differ in magnitude, not in shape.

Source · NASA FIRMS · MODIS + VIIRS · 2014–2024

How intense is the fire?

Brightness · Fire radiative power · Scan area · 2014–2024

Satellites measure fire in three simultaneous dimensions: brightness (the temperature of the pixel in Kelvin), fire radiative power (the energy flux in megawatts, derived from the 4 µm band), and scan area (the ground footprint of the sensor). The animation below builds those three dimensions tier by tier, revealing how different categories of NT fires occupy distinct regions of thermal intensity space.

Fire intensity

Brighter hotspots radiate more power — but footprint tells a different story.

Across a 900-point sample drawn from 757 000 NT hotspot records, brightness (K) and fire radiative power (FRP, MW) show a moderate positive correlation. The bulk of detections cluster in the Low tier — the dense cloud of grass fires that define the NT dry season landscape.

Large scan footprints are not confined to high-intensity events; many low-FRP fires cover broad areas because the sensor pixel spans more ground at wide view angles. Extreme-tier events are rare but disproportionately energetic — reinforcing that scan size alone is a poor proxy for fire danger. Use the “Detail:” filter to zoom each tier’s axes independently.

The preparedness gap

High-risk, zero measures.

Every single community in the Unprepared tier — no fuel reduction, no firebreak, no fire plan — is also unfunded. Of the 168 high-risk communities, 140 fall into this bracket. The Sankey below makes the structural pattern visible.

Three ridgelines, three very different stories. High-risk NT communities are overwhelmingly tiny — most house fewer than 100 people, placing them beyond the reach of rapid emergency response. Small populations mean less political voice, less infrastructure funding, and fewer trained local volunteers.

Moderate-risk communities span the widest range: from tiny seasonal outposts to small towns with some services, reflecting diverse land tenures and fire-management histories. Low-risk communities also skew small, but their classification reflects better preparedness and proximity to suppression resources — not just size.

Each dot is one community. Hover for name, LGA, population, and preparedness score. The yellow line marks the median; the shaded box spans the interquartile range.

How to read the Sankey

Follow the bands left to right.

The diagram has three columns of nodes. On the left, communities are grouped by their official risk level (Low, Moderate, High). In the centre, they flow into a preparedness tier based on how many of the three core measures they have in place: fuel reduction, firebreaks, and a fire plan. On the right, each tier connects to a funding status node — funded or not funded.

The width of each band is proportional to the number of communities it carries. A thick band from High Risk → Unprepared → Not Funded is the structural problem this dataset reveals. Hover any band or node for the exact community count.

The key finding

Every unprepared community receives no funding.

All 274 communities in the Unprepared (0/3) tier — those with no fuel reduction, no firebreaks, and no fire plan — end up in the “Not Funded” node. The funding system does not reach the communities that have done nothing, creating a reinforcing cycle: no measures means no funding, which means no capacity to implement measures.

Of the 168 communities rated High Risk, the majority flow through the Unprepared channel. Risk level and preparedness level are structurally misaligned across the NT.

Source · NT Community Bushfire Risk Assessment · 2020

Who is prepared?

Small multiples · 100 % stacked bars · four dimensions · 765 communities

Preparedness is not evenly distributed. By slicing the same 765-community dataset across risk level, community type, fire management zone, and regional council, a consistent picture emerges: the “Low (1/3)” tier dominates everywhere. Family Outstations and high-risk communities carry the greatest share of zero-measure households, while the barriers appear structural rather than geographic.

Preparedness

Most communities have only partial measures in place.

Across all four dimensions, the “Low (1/3)” tier dominates — meaning the majority of NT communities have adopted just one of the three core bushfire preparedness measures: fuel reduction, firebreaks, or a fire plan.

Family Outstations stand out with the highest share of fully unprepared communities. In contrast, Major communities show a broader spread, with a meaningful proportion reaching the “Full (3/3)” tier. The pattern holds across fire management zones and regional councils, suggesting structural rather than geographic barriers to preparedness.
